Pace of Play

Nobody likes to wait 15 minutes to hit their next shot, or play a round in a speedy 5 hours. You don't have to be a scratch golfer to play quickly - you just have to follow a few guidelines to save lots of time. The following are some tips on how to maintain a 4:15 pace.

  • Play ready golf!
    This is probably the most violated rule when it comes to maintaining a good pace of play. Too many golfers insist on waiting until they are away> before they hit their next shot. If you ar in the way of another golfer, by all means wait, but if you are not always but can hit your next shot without interfering with someone else, do it. This is true on the tee box, in the fairway or rough, and on the putting green. If you are 20 feet from the pin and your playing partner is 21 feet but is raking the trap, go ahead and putt - don't wait for him to finish raking.
  • Continuous putting!
    Another practice often violated. If you miss your first putt, finish putting out if you are not in the line of someone else. Most of the greens we play tend to be very hard and even if you step in someone else's line, you are not likely to make a dent in the green.
  • Keep your clubs handy!
    When you walk up to the green with several clubs, lay everything but the putter where you can pick it up on the way off the green - i.e. between you and the cart. Don't leave them on the other side of the green where you have to chase after them when you are done putting.
  • Keep score on the next tee!
    Don't stand aroung the green asking everybody what they shot on that hole and writing it down on the score card. Get in your cart and go to the next tee and then write down the scores.
  • Ignore honors!
    This isn't the US Amateur. When you get to the next tee box, go ahead and hit even if you just got a 10 on the previous hole. This is a corollary of ready golf.

Your Handicap

In almost every amateur golf competition, your HANDICAP plays a role in whether you win or lose. Handicaps come into play in two ways. Handicaps have been around (I have read) since the 17th century in Scotland. Perhaps the earliest form of handicapping was referred to as "First Tee Negotiation."

Handicaps come into play in two ways.

  1. Your handicap is subtracted from your 18-hole Gross Score (what you actually shoot on the course with no adjustments) to give you a Net Score (an adjustment recognizing the skill differences between two or more players). Net score is what most amateur club tournaments use to determine winners, with the obvious exception of tournaments like Club Championships. Even these tournaments usually have a major component based on Net Scores. Without them, most of us could not compete.
  2. Sometimes handicaps are applied to individual holes in addition to the 18-hole total. On the score card, every hole has a handicap rating from 1 to 18 to indicate the difficulty of that hole, with the #1 handicap hole being the most difficult (not exacly true but close enough for this discussion). If you have a 12 handicap and the hole is rated as the #8 handicap hole, you get a stoke on that hole, so if you shot a 5 then your Net Score on that hole would be 4. If the hole is rated as a 14, you do not get a stroke and your net score remains a 5. In competition, this hole by hole competition is referred to as Net Skins. Gross Skins are the same except without handicaps - a 5 is a 5. Basically there are 18 1-hole competitions within an 18 hole tournament in addition to the 18-hole Net (or Gross) Score competition.

The mystery of a handicap is primarily in the language used to explain it. Course Rating, Course Slope, Adjusted Gross Score, Score Differentials, and Index are all used to determine your handicap. Prior to 2020, there were as many as 6 different handicap systems in use around the world, with the USGA and the R&A (Royal and Ancient) being the most prominent. In 2020 they got together and had a meeting of the minds and created the World Handicap System (WHS) so that now all golfers worldwide have a common process for calculating handicaps making it more fair for everybody. Very few golfers really know how handicaps are determined - only that it is a reflection of their own skill level. The lower your handicap, he better golfer you (probably) are. With that in mind, the following is an explanation of the terms used to describe the handicapping process. The first two (Course Rating and Slope) quantify the difficulty of the course for both the scratch golfer and the bogey golfer.

  • Course Rating (CR): This is a number that represents what a scratch golfer should expect to shoot on average over a period of time. It is usually within a few shots of par (e.g. 73.2 for a difficult Par 72 course or 69.4 for an easier par 72 course). A key point here is that it is based on what the scratch golfer should expect to shoot. The course rating will be different for each set of tees and is usually found on the score card.
  • Course Slope (CS): Slope was added to the formula by the USGA in 1987 to account for the difficulty of a golf course for the bogey golfer (e.g. an 18 handicapper). It is a number between 55 and 155 with 113 meaning the same degree of difficulty for scratch and bogey golfers. The higher the slope, the more the course favors the scratch golfer (e.g. more hazards to avoid). The lower the slope the more it favors the bogey golfer (e.g. not so much trouble to avoid). Like course rating, the slope is different for each set of tees and is usually found on the score card along with the course rating.
  • Adjusted Gross Score (AGS): In 2020, when the WHS was created, the powers that be made a change to the maximum score a golfer could count on a hole for handicap purposes. Without going into the details of what it was before 2020, it is now a net double bogey. For example, if you get a 10 on a par 5 hole and get one shot, on that hole an 8 would give you a net double of 7. So, for handicap purposes, the most you can count on that hole is 8. You still have to count the 10 for every other purpose. Your adjusted gross score is now two shots less than the score recorded on your score card. For Grumpy Old Men, you don't have to make this adjustmemt, the handicapping sytstem does that for you when your hole by hole scores are entered into the system.
  • Score Differential: Simply put, this is the difference between your adjusted gross score and the course rating (AGS - CR). For example, if the course rating is 71.6 and your adjusted gross score is 86, the differential is 86 - 71.6 = 14.4. As you will see, these score differentials are part of the equation for calculating your Index which is part of the calculation for Course Handicap.
  • Index: This is the last number to be determined before calculating your course handicap. It's simple to calculate. For now, let's assume that you have entered 20 or more adjusted gross scores into the handicap system. There will be a Score Differential for each of these scores (e.g. 14.4). Your index is the average of the best (lowest) 8 score differentials from your most recent 20 scores.

    Why do we need an Index? Because it is portable. It is an indication of your golfing ability on easy or difficult courses. You can go to other courses and calculate your handicap based on the difficulty of that course.

  • Course Handicap (CH): FINALLY we get to what we all (or at least some of us I hope) wanted to know - how your course handicap is really determined. It all comes down to this formula:

    • CH = [(Index * Slope) / 113] + (CR - PAR).
